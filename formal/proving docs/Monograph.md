# Part I — Conceptual Foundations of the Classical Coherence Field Theory (CCFT)

## 1. Overview of CCFT as a Unified Classical Field Framework

The Classical Coherence Field Theory (CCFT) is a unified nonlinear classical field theory that integrates multiple dynamical subsystems into one coherent mathematical structure. CCFT combines:

1. The CP–NLSE first-order coherence-preserving nonlinear Schrödinger equation.
2. The CE–NWE second-order coherence-extended nonlinear wave equation.
3. The LCRD rotor–curvature subsystem describing local coherence distortions.
4. The φ–χ multi-field extension introducing additional coupled degrees of freedom.

Together, these subsystems describe density, velocity, coherence, dispersion, and structural distortions within a single nonlinear classical theory. CCFT is not a quantum theory. Its goal is to form a mesoscopic classical layer with tightly organized dynamics from which coherent structures, dispersive waves, and multi-field interactions emerge in a mathematically consistent manner.

## 2. Motivating Principles Behind CCFT

### 2.1 Coherence as a Dynamical Quantity

The governing idea of CCFT is that coherence is not a static property but a quantity that evolves dynamically in response to gradients, nonlinear interactions, dispersion, and microstructural distortions. The theory models how coherent features persist, decay, or transform across space and time.

### 2.2 Dispersion as the Unifying Mechanism

All CCFT subsystems share a common dispersion structure derived from LSDA microphysical calibration. This ensures that the first-order and second-order formulations are consistent with each other, the rotor–curvature dynamics remain compatible with the core equations, and the φ–χ extension does not introduce contradictory behaviors.

### 2.3 Structured Energy Functionals

Each subsystem is supported by a well-defined energy functional. These functionals determine allowable field configurations, stabilize the dynamics, and support theoretical and numerical closure. Quadratic energy terms appear in the rotor sector, kinetic and potential energy terms appear in second-order systems, and LSDA-derived nonlinear energy contributions appear in the CP–NLSE and CE–NWE branches.

## 3. Subsystem Foundations and Rationale

### 3.1 CP–NLSE Subsystem

#### Purpose
The CP–NLSE governs the primary complex coherence field. It controls density through the amplitude, velocity through the phase gradient, and nonlinear self-interaction through the LSDA-calibrated coupling \( g_{\mathrm{eff}} \).

#### Why a first-order equation
The first-order form is chosen because it provides accurate coherent transport, simple coupling to hydrodynamic variables, and stability under dispersion-dominated regimes.

#### Why λ = 0 and β = 0 in CCFT
These higher-order nonlinear corrections are intentionally omitted to preserve minimal LSDA-calibrated dynamics and avoid introducing additional dispersion or steepening terms that are not supported by microphysical analysis.

#### How LSDA determines \( g_{\mathrm{eff}} \)
LSDA computes effective nonlinear coupling from local structure–density relationships. This mapping directly produces the constant \( g_{\mathrm{eff}} \) that appears in both the first-order and second-order CCFT branches.

### 3.2 CE–NWE Subsystem

#### Purpose
CE–NWE is the second-order formulation equivalent to CP–NLSE. It captures inertial and oscillatory features that are less transparent in first-order form.

#### Why second-order dynamics matter
Second-order equations provide a classical interpretation of acceleration, wave propagation, and elastic-like responses. These behaviors become important when analyzing φ–χ interactions or rotor backreaction.

#### Why CE–NWE is not a different physical model
It is mathematically equivalent to CP–NLSE through standard transformations. Both share the same dispersion relation, invariants, and nonlinear structure.

#### How dispersion unifies both branches
Because the dispersion relation is shared, solitons, wave packets, modulational instability properties, and perturbative modes are identical across both formulations.

### 3.3 LCRD Subsystem

#### Purpose
The LCRD subsystem introduces the fields R and K to describe LSDA-identified microstructural distortions, specifically rotational coherence and curvature-like effects.

#### Why R and K exist
LSDA reveals coarse-grained microstructural features that cannot be captured by density and phase alone. R represents rotational coherence distortions, while K represents curvature or bending-like distortions.

#### Why rotor energy is quadratic
The form \( \frac{c_1}{2}R^2 + \frac{c_2}{2}K^2 \) provides the simplest energy penalty that stabilizes these fields and matches LSDA-derived microphysical energetics.

#### Why rotor effects modify momentum but not continuity
Mass conservation does not depend on microstructural distortion. Rotor effects influence momentum flux through a pressure-like correction term \( Q_{\mathrm{rotor}} \), but they do not alter continuity dynamics.

### 3.4 φ–χ Multi-Field Extension

#### Purpose
The φ–χ extension tests CCFT’s ability to support multi-field couplings while preserving stability and coherence properties. It introduces a new second-order field χ weakly coupled to the first-order φ field.

#### Why χ is included
The χ field models additional elastic-like or structural modes not representable by the single complex field φ.

#### Why coupling is weak
Weak coupling ensures that φ’s coherence-preserving structure remains intact while allowing χ to influence the system without destabilizing it.

#### Why χ is second-order while φ is first-order
φ carries coherent transport, while χ introduces inertial or restorative dynamics. This asymmetry reflects their distinct physical roles.

#### Why this preserves CCFT limits
When couplings vanish, φ returns to standard CP–NLSE/CE–NWE behavior and χ becomes a decoupled wave equation. The extension therefore generalizes rather than replaces CCFT.

## 4.2 CRFT Numerical Rules (Operators, Viscosity Mapping, Stability)

This section defines the standard numerical rules inherited from the CRFT simulation suite and reused by CCFT.

### 4.2.1 Fourier-Spectral Derivative Operators

Consider a periodic grid of length \(L\) with \(N\) points.

- **Spatial samples**
  \[
  x_j = \frac{L j}{N}, \quad j = 0, 1, \dots, N-1.
  \]

- **Wavenumbers**
  \[
  k = \frac{2\pi}{L}\,\mathrm{fftfreq}(N),
  \]
  where \(\mathrm{fftfreq}(N)\) is the standard discrete frequency array.

Given a field \(f(x)\) with Fourier transform \(\hat{f}(k)\), spectral derivatives are defined as:

- **First derivative**
  \[
  D_1 f = \mathcal{F}^{-1} \big[ (i k)\,\hat{f} \big].
  \]

- **Second derivative**
  \[
  D_2 f = \mathcal{F}^{-1} \big[ -k^2\,\hat{f} \big].
  \]

- **Fourth derivative**
  \[
  D_4 f = \mathcal{F}^{-1} \big[ k^4\,\hat{f} \big].
  \]

- **Sixth derivative**
  \[
  D_6 f = \mathcal{F}^{-1} \big[ -k^6\,\hat{f} \big].
  \]

These operators are used for \(\phi\), \(\chi\), \(R\), \(K\), and hydrodynamic reductions (e.g. derivatives of \(\rho\) and \(u\)).

### 4.2.2 Numerical Viscosity Mapping

CRFT supports an optional spectral numerical viscosity to stabilize high-\(k\) modes while preserving the physical dispersion of low-\(k\) modes. The viscosity function is:

\[
\nu(k) = \nu_0 \left( \frac{|k|}{k_{\max}} \right)^p,
\]

where:
- \(\nu_0\) is a base viscosity amplitude,
- \(k_{\max}\) is the maximum resolved wavenumber,
- \(p\) is typically \(4\) or \(6\), depending on the simulation.

This viscosity is applied additively to the evolution equation. For the φ field:

\[
\phi_t \;\rightarrow\; \phi_t - \nu(k)\,\phi,
\]

applied in Fourier space. The same mapping can be used for rotor fields \(R\) and \(K\) when needed, ensuring numerical stability while preserving low-\(k\) physics.

### 4.2.3 Time-Stepping Stability

A standard fourth-order Runge–Kutta (RK4) scheme is used for time integration. For CP–NLSE and CE–NWE branches, the timestep must satisfy:

\[
dt < \frac{C}{k_{\max}^2 + g_{\mathrm{eff}}\rho_0},
\]

with \(C \in [0.1, 0.5]\) depending on the chosen resolution and the desired stability margin.

For χ-sector simulations with strong second-order dynamics, the more restrictive condition:

\[
dt < \frac{C}{c_\chi k_{\max}}
\]

may apply. These constraints ensure that the fastest linear waves and the stiffest nonlinear contributions are stably resolved.

## 4.3 LSDA → CRFT Parameter Extraction

The LSDA layer defines the microphysical response coefficients that CCFT must reproduce at the coarse-grained level. This subsection describes how \(g_{\mathrm{eff}}\), rotor coefficients \(c_1, c_2\), and χ-sector parameters are extracted.

### 4.3.1 Extracting \(g_{\mathrm{eff}}\)

LSDA simulations provide an empirical dispersion relation for the primary coherence channel:

\[
\omega_{\mathrm{LSDA}}^2(k) = A k^2 + B k^4.
\]

The CRFT/CCFT analytic dispersion relation is:

\[
\omega(k)^2 = g_{\mathrm{eff}} \rho_0 k^2 + \frac{1}{4} k^4.
\]

Matching LSDA to CCFT yields:

\[
g_{\mathrm{eff}} = \frac{A}{\rho_0}, \qquad B \approx \frac{1}{4}.
\]

Thus LSDA determines \(A\), and CCFT adopts:

- \(g_{\mathrm{eff}} = A/\rho_0\),
- the quartic coefficient fixed at \(1/4\) to preserve the canonical form of the CRFT dispersive term.

This procedure determines the nonlinear coupling in all φ-equations within CCFT.

### 4.3.2 Rotor Coefficients \(c_1, c_2\)

The LCRD rotor subsystem depends on two coefficients \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) that govern the energetic cost of rotor excitations:

\[
E_{\mathrm{rotor}} = \frac{c_1}{2} R^2 + \frac{c_2}{2} K^2.
\]

These are obtained as follows:

- \(R\) is fitted to LSDA-measured shear alignment decay rates, ensuring that rotor relaxation in the reduced model matches microphysical relaxation.
- \(K\) is fitted to LSDA curvature-response dynamics, such as how quickly microstructure relaxes in regions of high curvature.

The fitted values of \(c_1\) and \(c_2\) are then used throughout CCFT to maintain compatibility with LSDA microphysics.

### 4.3.3 χ-Sector Parameters

The auxiliary χ field is calibrated so that:

- the linear term \(\gamma\) reproduces LSDA-measured curvature-relaxation times,
- the wave speed \(c_\chi\) matches the propagation speed of curvature-related microstructural disturbances,
- the coupling \(\alpha\) is chosen so that χ back-reaction modulates φ dynamics without destroying coherence.

This calibration ensures that the χ field acts as a physically interpretable curvature-responsive channel, rather than an arbitrary auxiliary field.

## 4.4 Full PDE Systems and Numerical Updates

This subsection lists the full PDE systems used in CCFT and the associated numerical update rules.

### 4.4.1 2D CP–NLSE Implementation (Full Block)

The baseline CCFT subsystem in two spatial dimensions is the CP–NLSE:

\[
i \phi_t = -\frac{1}{2} \nabla^2 \phi + g_{\mathrm{eff}} (|\phi|^2 - \rho_0)\,\phi.
\]

On a periodic 2D grid:

1. **Laplacian via spectral operator**

   Let \(k_x, k_y\) be the 2D wavenumber components. Then
   \[
   \nabla^2 \phi = \mathcal{F}^{-1} \big[ - (k_x^2 + k_y^2)\,\hat{\phi} \big].
   \]

2. **Nonlinear term**

   Compute
   \[
   N(\phi) = g_{\mathrm{eff}} (|\phi|^2 - \rho_0)\,\phi.
   \]

3. **Right-hand side for RK4**

   Rewrite the PDE as
   \[
   \phi_t = F(\phi) = -\,i \left( -\frac{1}{2} \nabla^2 \phi + N(\phi) \right).
   \]

4. **RK4 update**

   Advance \(\phi\) using standard RK4 steps with \(F(\phi)\).

5. **Diagnostics**

   At each diagnostic step, compute:
   \[
   N(t) = \int |\phi|^2 \, dx\,dy,
   \]
   \[
   E(t) = \int \left( \frac{1}{2} |\nabla \phi|^2 + \frac{g_{\mathrm{eff}}}{2} (|\phi|^2 - \rho_0)^2 \right) dx\,dy.
   \]

These diagnostics validate numerical conservation of norm and energy where appropriate.

### 4.4.2 LCRD v3 Rotor Subsystem

The LCRD rotor subsystem models curvature-induced coherence dynamics. It introduces two rotor fields:

- \(R\): alignment rotor,
- \(K\): curvature rotor.

A representative form of the rotor evolution equations is:

\[
R_t = a_1 D_1(\rho u) + a_2 D_2 \rho + S_R(\phi),
\]
\[
K_t = b_1 D_2 u + b_2 D_3 \rho + S_K(\phi),
\]

where:

- \(D_1, D_2, D_3\) are spectral derivative operators,
- \(S_R(\phi)\), \(S_K(\phi)\) are φ-dependent source terms calibrated against LSDA,
- \(\rho\) and \(u\) are the hydrodynamic density and velocity derived from φ.

The rotor energy density is:

\[
E_{\mathrm{rotor}} = \frac{c_1}{2} R^2 + \frac{c_2}{2} K^2.
\]

The associated rotor pressure correction entering the momentum equation is:

\[
Q_{\mathrm{rotor}} = c_1 \partial_x R + c_2 \partial_{xx} K.
\]

This term modifies the momentum equation but does not alter the continuity equation. This design ensures that mass conservation is preserved while momentum flux receives curvature-induced corrections.

**Rotor stability diagnostics** include:

- boundedness of \(R\) and \(K\) over long times,
- decay of high-\(k\) rotor spectral components when physical conditions demand relaxation,
- consistency between rotor-induced corrections and LSDA curvature-response behavior.

The rotor subsystem is advanced using RK4 and the same spectral operators as the φ field.

### 4.4.3 φ–χ Extended CRFT PDE System

The φ–χ extension introduces a weakly coupled second field χ that responds to curvature and modulates φ.

The coupled system is:

- **φ-equation**:
  \[
  i \phi_t
  = -\frac{1}{2} \nabla^2 \phi
    + g_{\mathrm{eff}} (|\phi|^2 - \rho_0)\,\phi
    + \alpha \chi \phi.

- **χ-equation (second-order)**:
  \[
  \chi_{tt} - c_\chi^2 \nabla^2 \chi + \gamma \chi = \alpha |\phi|^2.

For numerical implementation, the χ equation is written as a first-order system:

- Define \(v = \chi_t\).
- Then
  \[
  \chi_t = v,
  \]
  \[
  v_t = c_\chi^2 \nabla^2 \chi - \gamma \chi + \alpha |\phi|^2.
  \]

The numerical updates proceed as follows:

1. Compute \(\nabla^2 \chi\) spectrally.
2. Form right-hand sides for \(\phi_t\), \(\chi_t\), and \(v_t\).
3. Advance the coupled system \((\phi, \chi, v)\) using RK4.

**Diagnostics**:

- χ-sector energy:
  \[
  E_\chi = \int \left[
    \frac{1}{2} v^2
    + \frac{c_\chi^2}{2} |\nabla \chi|^2
    + \frac{\gamma}{2} \chi^2
  \right] dx.
  \]

- φ–χ interaction energy:
  \[
  E_{\mathrm{int}} = \int \alpha \chi |\phi|^2 \, dx.
  \]

Weak coupling (\(\alpha\) small) ensures that the extension perturbs but does not destroy the core CRFT/CCFT structure and limits.

E_R(k) = |\hat{R}(k)|^2, \qquad
E_K(k) = |\hat{K}(k)|^2.

These reveal how rotor energy is distributed across scales and how it evolves in time, providing insight into multiscale curvature dynamics.

# Limitations

This subsection identifies the explicit limitations of the Classical Coherence Field Theory (CCFT) as currently formulated. * quantum operators,
* probabilistic postulates,
* Planck-scale parameters,
* renormalization procedures,
* canonical quantization.

Any quantum interpretation must be derived from CCFT’s structural properties and is not assumed by the theory itself.

All governing equations, derivations, and validations have been carried out in one spatial dimension. This simplifies analysis and ensures numerical stability but introduces constraints:

* coherent structures may behave differently in higher dimensions,
* rotor–curvature definitions may require additional components,
* auxiliary-field interactions may generalize non-trivially.

The effective nonlinear coefficient g\_eff = 9.8696 and the rotor–curvature coefficients are derived from LSDA behavior. CCFT’s accuracy therefore depends on:

* the correctness of LSDA as a microphysical model,
* the fidelity of LSDA-derived sound-speed and coherence measurements,
* the assumption that LSDA microstructures are representative.

If LSDA is refined or replaced, calibration constants would need reassessment.

## 4\. Rotor–Curvature Subsystem is Mesoscopic

The R and K fields summarize LSDA-observed microstructure but are not fundamental physical fields. Their governing equations:

* approximate coarse-grain coherence dynamics,
* omit higher-order nonlinear interactions,
* capture only leading-order mesoscopic corrections.

The subsystem is intentionally simple to preserve analytical and numerical tractability.

## 5\. χ-Field Extension is Preliminary

Although χ provides a consistent multi-field extension, the subsystem remains exploratory:

* χ-terms are limited to even-order spatial derivatives,
* the coupling α and γ are weak by assumption,
* χ has not been tested across all possible parameter regimes,
* higher-dimensional and strong-coupling behaviors remain unknown.

The φ–χ subsystem demonstrates extensibility but is not a final multi-field model.

## 6\. No Complete Lagrangian or Hamiltonian Formulation Yet

While partial energy functionals exist, CCFT currently lacks:

* a single unified action functional for all fields,
* consistent canonical momenta for φ and χ,
* a fully variational derivation of the rotor–curvature subsystem.

Such a formulation is essential for eventual quantization and remains unfinished.

## 7\. No Relativistic Embedding Yet

Although CCFT supports:

* hydrodynamic acoustic metrics,
* geometric analogues via rotor–curvature terms,
* second-order wave dynamics,

it does not yet include:

* Lorentz invariance,
* covariant derivatives,
* spacetime curvature as a dynamical variable.

Embedding CCFT into a relativistic formalism is beyond current scope.

## 8\. Limited Experimental or Empirical Connection

CCFT is derived from computational observations (LSDA) and validated through numerical tests (C1–C13, L1–L10, extended multi-field regimes). At present:

* there is no direct empirical dataset known to correspond to CCFT,
* parameters are calibrated computationally, not experimentally,
* predictions are theoretical until matched to measurable systems.

CCFT’s physical relevance relies on future empirical mapping.

\### A.1.2 Euler–Lagrange Variation



Apply the Euler–Lagrange equation for complex fields:



\\\[

\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial \\phi^\*}

-\\partial\_t\\left(\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial \\phi\_t^\*}\\right)

-\\partial\_i\\left(\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial (\\partial\_i\\phi^\*)}\\right)

=0.

\\]



Compute the derivatives:



\\\[

\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial\\phi^\*}

= \\frac{i}{2}\\phi\_t - g\_{\\mathrm{eff}}(|\\phi|^2 - \\rho\_0)\\phi,

\\]



\\\[

\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial\\phi\_t^\*}

= -\\frac{i}{2}\\phi,

\\qquad

\\partial\_t\\left(\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial\\phi\_t^\*}\\right)

= -\\frac{i}{2}\\phi\_t,

\\]



\\\[

\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial(\\nabla\\phi^\*)}

= -\\frac{1}{2}\\nabla\\phi,

\\qquad

\\nabla\\cdot\\frac{\\partial\\mathcal{L}}{\\partial(\\nabla\\phi^\*)}

= -\\frac{1}{2}\\nabla^2\\phi.

\\]



Combine all terms:



\\\[

i\\phi\_t = -\\frac{1}{2}\\nabla^2\\phi

          + g\_{\\mathrm{eff}}(|\\phi|^2 - \\rho\_0)\\phi.

\\]



This is the CCFT CP–NLSE.
